## Purpose

This document summarizes the material selection rationale for AMPEL360 BWB primary structure components, documenting material choices, properties, and justification based on structural requirements, environmental conditions, and lifecycle considerations.

## Scope

Material selection covers:
- Carbon fiber composites
- Aluminum alloys
- Titanium alloys
- Steel alloys
- Hybrid material systems
- Material qualification and certification

## Material Selection Criteria

### Key Selection Factors

1. **Structural Performance**
   - Strength-to-weight ratio
   - Stiffness-to-weight ratio
   - Fatigue resistance
   - Damage tolerance
   - Buckling resistance

2. **Environmental Compatibility**
   - Corrosion resistance
   - Temperature range
   - Humidity and moisture effects
   - UV exposure
   - Chemical compatibility (fuel, fluids, H₂)

3. **Manufacturing Considerations**
   - Fabrication processes available
   - Quality control and inspection
   - Assembly and joining methods
   - Tolerances achievable
   - Cost and lead time

4. **Lifecycle Factors**
   - Inspection and NDT methods
   - Repair techniques and approvals
   - Spare parts availability
   - Recycling and disposal
   - Total cost of ownership

5. **Certification and Qualification**
   - Material specifications (AMS, ASTM, etc.)
   - Test data and allowables
   - Environmental qualification
   - Regulatory acceptance (EASA, FAA)

## Primary Material Systems

### 1. Carbon Fiber Reinforced Polymer (CFRP)

**Applications**:
- Upper and lower fuselage skins
- Stringers and stiffeners
- Wing-body fairings
- Non-critical bulkheads

**Material Specifications**:
- **Fiber**: Toray T800, Hexcel IM7, or equivalent (intermediate modulus carbon)
- **Resin**: Epoxy (Hexcel 8552, Cytec 977-3, or equivalent)
- **Forms**: Unidirectional tape, woven fabric, prepreg

**Properties** (Typical for IM7/8552 system):
- **Tensile Strength**: 2,800 MPa (fiber direction)
- **Tensile Modulus**: 160 GPa (fiber direction)
- **Density**: 1.58 g/cm³
- **Operating Temperature**: -55°C to +120°C (continuous)

**Advantages**:
- Excellent strength-to-weight ratio (~5× better than aluminum)
- Superior fatigue resistance (no fatigue limit for compression)
- Corrosion immunity
- Tailorable properties (layup optimization)

**Challenges**:
- Impact damage susceptibility (delamination, fiber breakage)
- Moisture absorption (up to 1% by weight)
- Repair complexity (requires specialized techniques)
- Cost (material and processing)
- Lightning strike protection required (conductive mesh, foils)

**Certification Basis**:
- Material qualification per [CS-25.603](https://www.easa.europa.eu/en/document-library/certification-specifications/cs-25-amendment-27)
- Composite Material Handbook (CMH-17) allowables
- Environmental durability testing (hot/wet conditions)

**Justification for BWB**:
- Weight savings critical for large structure
- Smooth contours easier with composites (fewer joints)
- Excellent for distributed load environments

### 2. Aluminum Alloys

**Applications**:
- Frames (circumferential and bulkheads)
- Floor beams and supports
- Access panels and removable structure
- Repair doublers

**Material Specifications**:
- **Primary Alloys**: 
  - 2024-T3 (high strength, good fatigue)
  - 7075-T6/T73 (very high strength)
  - 7050-T7451 (damage tolerance, thick sections)
  - 2198-T8 (Al-Li, improved properties)

**Properties** (Typical for 7075-T6):
- **Tensile Strength**: 572 MPa
- **Yield Strength**: 503 MPa
- **Modulus**: 71 GPa
- **Density**: 2.81 g/cm³
- **Elongation**: 11%

**Advantages**:
- Mature technology (extensive database, proven track record)
- Excellent damage tolerance (ductile, crack growth predictable)
- Easy to inspect (visual, NDT)
- Field-repairable (conventional tools and techniques)
- Cost-effective for moderate production volumes

**Challenges**:
- Weight penalty vs. composites (~60% heavier for same strength)
- Corrosion susceptibility (requires protection systems)
- Fatigue cracks (require inspection programs)

**Corrosion Protection**:
- Anodizing (MIL-A-8625)
- Primer and topcoat (MIL-PRF-23377, MIL-PRF-85285)
- Sealants and barriers at interfaces
- Corrosion inhibiting compounds (CIC) in joints

**Certification Basis**:
- MMPDS (Metallic Materials Properties Development and Standardization) allowables
- ASTM material specifications
- Fatigue crack growth data per [CS-25.571](https://www.easa.europa.eu/en/document-library/certification-specifications/cs-25-amendment-27)

**Justification for BWB**:
- Frames benefit from ductility and repairability
- Lower cost for secondary and tertiary structure
- Proven performance in pressurized fuselage applications

### 3. Titanium Alloys

**Applications**:
- Landing gear attachment fittings
- Wing attachment fittings
- High-temperature areas (near engines)
- Highly-loaded pins and bushings

**Material Specifications**:
- **Primary Alloy**: Ti-6Al-4V (Grade 5)
- **Forms**: Forgings, plate, bar

**Properties** (Ti-6Al-4V, annealed):
- **Tensile Strength**: 930 MPa
- **Yield Strength**: 860 MPa
- **Modulus**: 114 GPa
- **Density**: 4.43 g/cm³
- **Operating Temperature**: -270°C to +300°C

**Advantages**:
- High strength-to-weight ratio (better than steel, not as good as CFRP)
- Excellent corrosion resistance (passive oxide layer)
- High-temperature capability
- Good fatigue resistance
- Compatible with aluminum and composites (minimal galvanic corrosion)

**Challenges**:
- High cost (material and machining)
- Difficult to machine (work-hardening, low thermal conductivity)
- Galling tendency (careful surface treatment required)

**Certification Basis**:
- AMS specifications (AMS 4928 for Ti-6Al-4V annealed)
- MMPDS allowables
- Fatigue and fracture toughness data

**Justification for BWB**:
- Critical fittings require high strength and reliability
- Temperature resistance near propulsion systems
- Weight savings vs. steel in large fittings

### 4. Steel Alloys

**Applications**:
- Fasteners (bolts, screws, rivets)
- Highly-loaded pins and bushings
- Landing gear components (if not titanium)

**Material Specifications**:
- **High-Strength Steels**: 
  - 300M (ultra-high strength)
  - 4340 (high strength, tough)
  - 17-4PH stainless (corrosion resistance)
  - A286 (high-temperature applications)

**Properties** (4340, heat-treated):
- **Tensile Strength**: 1,790 MPa
- **Yield Strength**: 1,655 MPa
- **Modulus**: 205 GPa
- **Density**: 7.85 g/cm³

**Advantages**:
- Very high strength (highest of all structural materials)
- Excellent bearing strength
- Well-characterized properties
- Cost-effective for small parts (fasteners)

**Challenges**:
- Heavy (density ~3× aluminum)
- Corrosion susceptibility (requires plating or coating)
- Brittleness risk if improperly heat-treated

**Certification Basis**:
- SAE and ASTM specifications for fasteners
- MMPDS allowables for structural steels
- Plating specifications for corrosion protection

**Justification for BWB**:
- Essential for high-load fasteners and pins
- Small quantity minimizes weight penalty
- Proven reliability in aerospace applications

## Material Distribution in BWB Structure

### Fuselage Zones

**Zone 1: Upper Fuselage (Wing Upper Surface)**
- **Primary Material**: CFRP skins and stringers
- **Rationale**: Compression under positive lift, CFRP excellent for buckling resistance

**Zone 2: Lower Fuselage (Wing Lower Surface)**
- **Primary Material**: CFRP skins and stringers
- **Rationale**: Tension under positive lift, CFRP high strength, fatigue resistance

**Zone 3: Side Fuselage**
- **Primary Material**: CFRP skins and stringers
- **Rationale**: Combined pressure and flight loads, CFRP optimal weight solution

**Zone 4: Frames and Bulkheads**
- **Primary Material**: Aluminum alloy (7075-T6, 7050-T7)
- **Rationale**: Hoop tension from pressure, benefit from aluminum ductility and repairability

**Zone 5: Floor Structure**
- **Primary Material**: Aluminum alloy (2024-T3 for beams, 7075 for seat tracks)
- **Rationale**: Impact and crash loads, aluminum damage tolerance critical

**Zone 6: Landing Gear Attachments**
- **Primary Material**: Titanium alloy (Ti-6Al-4V) or high-strength steel
- **Rationale**: Very high concentrated loads, titanium optimal strength-to-weight

**Zone 7: Wing Attachments**
- **Primary Material**: Titanium alloy forgings
- **Rationale**: Critical load path, high reliability required

**Zone 8: Access Panels and Removable Structure**
- **Primary Material**: Aluminum alloy (2024-T3)
- **Rationale**: Frequent removal, ease of fabrication and replacement

## Hybrid Material Interfaces

### CFRP-to-Aluminum Joints

**Challenges**:
- Galvanic corrosion (carbon as cathode, aluminum as anode)
- Thermal expansion mismatch (CFRP: ~0.5 ppm/°C, Al: ~23 ppm/°C)
- Load transfer and stress concentration

**Design Solutions**:
- **Isolation**: Glass fiber buffer layer or non-conductive interlayer
- **Sealants**: Barrier sealants to prevent moisture ingress
- **Fasteners**: Titanium or stainless steel (not aluminum)
- **Coatings**: Anodize aluminum, prime all surfaces

### CFRP-to-Titanium Joints

**Advantages**:
- Minimal galvanic corrosion
- Good thermal expansion compatibility (Ti: ~8.6 ppm/°C)

**Design Approach**:
- Direct bolted connections acceptable
- Sealants for environmental protection
- Standard aerospace fasteners

## Material Environmental Qualification

### Testing Requirements

Per [CS-25.603](https://www.easa.europa.eu/en/document-library/certification-specifications/cs-25-amendment-27), materials must be qualified for:

1. **Temperature Range**: -55°C to +120°C (ambient operating conditions)
2. **Humidity**: 85% RH (relative humidity) exposure
3. **Fluids**: Resistance to fuels, oils, hydraulic fluids, cleaning agents, H₂
4. **UV Exposure**: For external surfaces
5. **Salt Fog**: Corrosion resistance testing (ASTM B117)

### Hot/Wet Conditions

**CFRP Composites**:
- Moisture absorption reduces properties (~10-15% reduction in compression strength)
- Design allowables based on hot/wet properties (conservative)
- Environmental conditioning of test specimens

**Aluminum Alloys**:
- Temperature reduces strength (high-temp allowables used)
- Corrosion accelerated in hot/humid environments
- Protection systems validated in environmental testing

## Sustainability and Lifecycle Considerations

### Recyclability

**Aluminum**:
- Highly recyclable (~95% of aluminum recycled)
- Energy savings: 95% less energy than primary production
- Well-established recycling infrastructure

**CFRP**:
- Recycling challenging (fiber-resin separation difficult)
- Emerging technologies: pyrolysis, chemical recycling
- Currently: downcycling (use in lower-grade applications) or energy recovery

**Titanium**:
- Recyclable, but expensive and energy-intensive
- Often recycled into lower-grade applications

### Design for End-of-Life

- **Material labeling**: Identify material types for disassembly
- **Fastened joints**: Prefer bolted over bonded for disassembly
- **Material segregation**: Design for separation of material types
- **Hazardous materials**: Minimize use of hazardous materials (e.g., hexavalent chromium)

## Material Traceability and Quality Control

### Material Certification

All materials supplied with:
- **Material Test Reports (MTR)**: Chemical composition, mechanical properties
- **Lot traceability**: Batch numbers traceable to source
- **Conformity certificates**: Compliance with specifications

### Incoming Inspection

- Verify certifications and traceability
- Visual inspection for damage or defects
- Sampling for verification testing (if required by quality plan)

### Storage and Handling

- **CFRP Prepreg**: Freezer storage (-18°C), limited shelf life (12 months typical)
- **Aluminum**: Ambient storage, protect from moisture and contamination
- **Titanium**: Ambient storage, prevent contamination (especially hydrogen embrittlement sources)
