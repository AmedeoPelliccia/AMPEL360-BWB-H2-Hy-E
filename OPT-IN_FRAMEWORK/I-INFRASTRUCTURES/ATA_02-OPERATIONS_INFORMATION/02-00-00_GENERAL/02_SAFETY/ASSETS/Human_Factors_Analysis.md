## 1. WORKLOAD ASSESSMENT BY FLIGHT PHASE

### 1.1 Taxi Out / Takeoff (HIGH WORKLOAD)

**Task Analysis:**
- Checklists: Pre-takeoff, line-up
- Coordination: ATC clearances, pushback, taxi clearance
- Monitoring: H2 system status, fuel cell performance, traffic
- BWB Specific: Wing tip clearance awareness, ground handling

**Workload Peaks:**
- Line-up checklist + takeoff clearance (simultaneous)
- Rotation: Tail strike awareness + performance monitoring
- Initial climb: Configuration changes + traffic avoidance

**Mitigation Strategies:**
- Early checklist completion when possible
- CAOS assists with H2/fuel cell monitoring
- Clear role division (PF flies, PM handles radios/checklists)
- Use of flow patterns to reduce checklist length

**Workload Rating:** 7/10 (High)

### 1.2 Climb / Cruise (MODERATE WORKLOAD)

**Task Analysis:**
- Monitoring: Flight parameters, systems, weather
- Planning: Fuel management, routing, weather ahead
- Communication: Position reports, company updates
- CAOS Interaction: Review recommendations, optimization

**Workload Characteristics:**
- Sustained moderate attention required
- Good time for system familiarization
- Planning for approach and landing
- H2/fuel cell long-term performance monitoring

**Mitigation Strategies:**
- Use autopilot and CAOS for routine tasks
- Divide tasks between crew (one monitors, one plans)
- Regular EICAS scans (every 5 minutes recommended)
- Breaks for physiological needs (one at a time)

**Workload Rating:** 4/10 (Moderate)

### 1.3 Descent / Approach / Landing (VERY HIGH WORKLOAD)

**Task Analysis:**
- Checklists: Descent, approach, landing
- Coordination: ATC instructions, approach clearances
- Monitoring: Performance, configuration, H2 system
- Flying: Precise flight path control, BWB handling
- Decision-making: Go-around assessment continuous

**Workload Peaks:**
- Descent checklist + ATC coordination + weather evaluation
- Final approach: Configuration + monitoring + flying
- Landing: Tail strike awareness + crosswind handling + speed control

**Mitigation Strategies:**
- Complete checklists early in descent
- Brief approach thoroughly including go-around plan
- CAOS provides enhanced monitoring alerts
- Use of automation until final approach (as appropriate)
- Sterile cockpit (no non-essential communication)

**Workload Rating:** 9/10 (Very High)

### 1.4 Abnormal / Emergency (EXTREME WORKLOAD)

**Task Analysis:**
- Diagnosis: Identify problem quickly and correctly
- Immediate Actions: Memory items, critical actions
- Checklists: QRH procedures (multiple may apply)
- Coordination: ATC (emergency declare), cabin crew, company
- Planning: Diversion, landing site, passenger preparation
- Flying: May have degraded flight characteristics

**Workload Peaks:**
- Initial recognition + immediate actions + ATC coordination
- Multiple system failures (compound emergencies)
- Final approach with degraded systems

**Mitigation Strategies:**
- **AVIATE - NAVIGATE - COMMUNICATE** priority
- Task shedding: Defer non-critical items
- Clear crew coordination: "I have controls", "You have radios"
- Use of CAOS for system monitoring (if functional)
- Request ATC assistance (vectors, priority, etc.)
- FOR-DEC decision model (see Section 4)

**Workload Rating:** 10/10 (Extreme)

## 2. FATIGUE RISK FACTORS AND INDICATORS

### 2.1 Operational Fatigue Risk Factors

**Circadian Disruption:**
- **High Risk Hours:** 02:00-06:00 local (body's low point)
- **Multiple Time Zone Crossings:** >4 time zones increases risk
- **Quick Reversals:** Day-night-day within 48 hours
- **Early Report Times:** <06:00 local disrupts sleep

**Sleep Debt:**
- **Acute:** <6 hours sleep previous night
- **Cumulative:** Multiple nights of reduced sleep
- **Sleep Quality:** Interrupted sleep less restorative

**Duty Time:**
- **Long Duty Days:** >12 hours increases risk significantly
- **Multiple Sectors:** >4 sectors/day increases fatigue
- **Standby Duty:** Uncertainty prevents good rest

**Environmental:**
- **Noise:** Airport hotels, crew rest facilities
- **Light Exposure:** Bright light before attempting sleep
- **Temperature:** Too warm or too cold impairs sleep

### 2.2 Fatigue Indicators (Self-Assessment)

**Physical Signs:**
- Heavy eyelids, frequent yawning
- Difficulty focusing eyes
- Head nodding
- Micro-sleeps (eyes close briefly)

**Cognitive Signs:**
- Difficulty concentrating
- Forgetfulness
- Slowed reaction time
- Confusion or disorientation

**Emotional Signs:**
- Irritability
- Mood changes
- Reduced motivation
- Loss of situational awareness

**Performance Signs:**
- Increased errors
- Missed radio calls
- Incomplete checklists
- Fixation on one task

### 2.3 Fatigue Countermeasures

**Pre-Flight:**
- Obtain adequate sleep (7-9 hours recommended)
- Maintain consistent sleep schedule
- Avoid alcohol before duty
- Strategic caffeine use (morning, not late)
- Light exposure management (bright light to wake up)

**In-Flight:**
- Caffeine strategic use (no later than 4 hours before planned sleep)
- Light snacks (avoid heavy meals)
- Hydration (water, not excessive caffeine)
- Physical movement (stretch, walk when safe)
- Environmental control (cockpit temperature, lighting)
- Controlled rest (if approved and procedures followed)

**Post-Flight:**
- Prioritize sleep before next duty
- Dark, quiet, cool sleep environment
- Avoid "nightcap" alcohol (disrupts sleep quality)
- Melatonin use (consult AME, especially for time zone shifts)

**Company Responsibilities:**
- Realistic scheduling
- Fatigue risk management system (FRMS)
- Education and training
- Non-punitive fatigue reporting
- Data analysis and schedule optimization

## 3. CRM PRINCIPLES APPLICATION

### 3.1 Communication (Inquiry-Advocacy-Assertion)

**Inquiry:** Ask questions to understand
- "What's your assessment of the H2 leak level?"
- "Help me understand why CAOS is recommending this route?"

**Advocacy:** State your position clearly
- "I recommend we divert to ALTERNATE due to fuel cell degradation."
- "I believe we should evacuate based on the fire indications."

**Assertion:** Speak up if safety concern
- "I'm not comfortable with that approach; weather is below our minimums."
- "STOP - I need to verify this before we proceed."

**BWB-CAOS Specific:**
- Clear communication of CG status
- CAOS recommendations discussed before implementation
- H2 system status verbalized
- Tail strike risk called out

### 3.2 Teamwork and Task Distribution

**Pilot Flying (PF) Primary Tasks:**
- Aircraft control (manual or monitoring automation)
- Flight path management
- Configuration changes
- Calls for checklists
- Final decision authority

**Pilot Monitoring (PM) Primary Tasks:**
- Radio communications (ATC)
- Checklist execution
- System monitoring (EICAS, CAOS)
- Navigation monitoring
- Performance calculations

**Dynamic Reallocation:**
- Workload sharing when one crew member saturated
- "I have the radios, you fly" or vice versa
- Clear verbal handoffs
- Monitor for task overload in partner

### 3.3 Decision-Making (FOR-DEC Model)

**F - Facts:** What do we know for certain?
- H2 leak detected, currently 3% LEL
- Fuel cell #2 is offline
- 450 nm from destination, 200 nm from alternate

**O - Options:** What can we do?
1. Continue to destination with monitoring
2. Divert to nearest alternate
3. Return to departure airport

**R - Risks & Benefits:** Evaluate each option
| Option | Benefits | Risks |
|--------|----------|-------|
| Continue | Destination facilities | Leak may worsen, longer flight |
| Divert | Shorter flight, less risk time | Unfamiliar airport, less support |
| Return | Known airport, company support | Overweight landing, longer in air |

**D - Decide:** Choose best option based on analysis
- Decision: Divert to alternate (best risk/benefit)

**E - Execute:** Implement the decision
- Declare emergency to ATC
- Brief cabin crew
- Execute diversion checklist
- Notify company

**C - Check:** Monitor outcome
- Is leak stable or worsening?
- Is plan working as expected?
- Adjust if needed

**CAOS Role:** Provides data for Facts, suggests Options, assesses Risks - but crew Decides.

### 3.4 Situational Awareness

**Three Levels of SA:**

**Level 1 - Perception:** What's happening?
- Monitoring displays, alerts, outside view
- Current aircraft state, systems status
- Immediate environment (weather, traffic)

**Level 2 - Comprehension:** What does it mean?
- Understanding significance of information
- Integrating multiple data sources
- Recognizing patterns or trends

**Level 3 - Projection:** What will happen next?
- Anticipating future states
- Planning ahead
- Predicting outcomes of actions

**SA Loss Indicators:**
- Confusion about aircraft state
- Surprise by events
- Fixation on one instrument/problem
- Loss of "big picture"

**SA Enhancement Strategies:**
- Regular "SA checks" ("Where are we? What's our plan?")
- Verbalize observations and intentions
- Cross-check between crew
- Use of CAOS for trend projection
- Avoid tunnel vision (scan widely)

## 4. AUTOMATION INTERACTION (CAOS)

### 4.1 Appropriate Trust in CAOS

**Trust Calibration Matrix:**

| CAOS Reliability | Crew Trust Level | Behavior |
|-----------------|------------------|----------|
| Consistently accurate (95%+) | Appropriate | Use with monitoring |
| Mostly accurate (85-95%) | Slightly cautious | Verify critical items |
| Sometimes inaccurate (<85%) | Low trust | Manual primary, CAOS advisory |
| Untested domain | Very low trust | Manual only, learn from CAOS |

**Over-Trust Indicators:**
- Accepting recommendations without review
- Not monitoring CAOS actions
- Surprise when CAOS makes error
- Inability to operate without CAOS

**Under-Trust Indicators:**
- Rejecting all CAOS recommendations
- Not using CAOS capabilities
- Excessive manual workload
- Ignoring CAOS alerts

**Calibration Strategies:**
- Training on CAOS capabilities AND limitations
- Experience with CAOS performance
- Regular manual flying (maintain proficiency)
- Understand CAOS confidence levels
- Review CAOS recommendations with crew

### 4.2 Mode Awareness

**CAOS Modes:**
- **FULL**: All systems operational, full advisory
- **DEGRADED**: Some systems unavailable, limited advisory
- **MONITORING ONLY**: CAOS monitors but no recommendations
- **OFFLINE**: Manual operations only

**Mode Confusion Prevention:**
- Clear, prominent mode indication on EICAS
- CAOS narrates actions ("I am recommending X because Y")
- Mode changes announced to crew
- Training emphasizes mode awareness
- No automatic mode changes without crew alert

**Mode Transition Triggers:**
- System failures (auto-transition to degraded/offline)
- Crew selection (manual mode change)
- Flight phase (some functions limited in takeoff/landing)
- Abnormal situations (may revert to manual)

### 4.3 Override Decision Criteria

**When to Override CAOS:**
1. **Safety Concern**: Recommendation seems unsafe
2. **Better Information**: Crew has information CAOS lacks
3. **Low Confidence**: CAOS confidence level <70%
4. **Regulatory**: Recommendation violates regulation
5. **Procedure**: Conflicts with company procedures
6. **Intuition**: "It doesn't feel right" (investigate, then decide)

**Override Procedure:**
- Single-action override capability
- CAOS acknowledges override
- Manual action takes precedence
- Event logged for review
- No negative consequences for appropriate override

**Reference:** [CAOS_Safety_Integration.md Section 2](../CAOS_Safety_Integration.md#2-human-ai-collaboration-safety)

### 4.4 Manual Skills Maintenance

**Flying Skills:**
- Regular manual flying (every flight, some portion)
- Practice unusual attitudes (simulator)
- Hand-flying approaches (periodically)
- Crosswind landings (manual)
- Emergency procedures (manual reversion)

**Mental Skills:**
- Manual flight planning exercises
- Weight & balance by hand (periodically)
- Performance calculations without CAOS
- Decision-making without AI assistance

**Company Requirements:**
- Minimum manual flying hours per month
- Simulator training includes CAOS failures
- Proficiency checks include manual-only scenarios

## 5. ERROR PREVENTION AND DETECTION

### 5.1 Common Error Types

**Skill-Based Errors (Slips and Lapses):**
- Intended action, incorrect execution
- Examples: Mis-selecting frequency, wrong switch
- Often during routine, automatic tasks
- Fatigue and distraction increase risk

**Rule-Based Errors:**
- Misapplication of a procedure or rule
- Example: Using wrong checklist for situation
- Incorrect pattern recognition
- Similar situations confused

**Knowledge-Based Errors:**
- Incorrect problem-solving
- Example: Misdiagnosis of system failure
- Occur in novel, non-routine situations
- Time pressure increases risk

### 5.2 Error-Prone Situations

**High-Risk Situations:**
- High workload (approach, emergency)
- Fatigue or illness
- Distractions or interruptions
- Time pressure
- Non-standard situations
- Poor communication
- Insufficient information

**AMPEL360-Specific:**
- H2 system anomalies (novel, may misdiagnose)
- BWB handling (different from conventional)
- CAOS recommendations (automation bias)
- Multiple system interactions

### 5.3 Error Prevention Strategies

**Individual:**
- Use of checklists and procedures
- Verbalize actions (especially critical ones)
- Cross-check before critical actions
- Take time for important decisions
- Recognize personal limitations (fatigue, stress)

**Team:**
- Inquiry-Advocacy-Assertion communication
- Challenge and respond to challenges
- Backup and cross-monitoring
- Speak up if you see error
- Two-person checks for critical items

**Organizational:**
- Well-designed procedures
- Effective training
- Fatigue risk management
- Just culture (learn from errors)
- System design (error-resistant)

### 5.4 Error Detection and Recovery

**Detection Methods:**
- Self-detection (recognize own error)
- Peer detection (crew member notices)
- System alerting (CAOS, EICAS alerts)
- Outcome monitoring (did action achieve intent?)

**Recovery Actions:**
1. **Recognize:** Identify that error occurred
2. **Stop:** Halt current action if needed
3. **Assess:** Evaluate consequences
4. **Correct:** Take corrective action
5. **Learn:** Understand why error occurred

**Error Management (TEM):**
- **Threat Management**: Anticipate and mitigate threats before they cause errors
- **Error Management**: Detect and correct errors before they lead to adverse outcomes
- **Undesired Aircraft State Management**: Recover from UAS before they lead to incidents

**Reference:** [Human_Factors_Operations.md Section 5](../Human_Factors_Operations.md#5-error-management)

## 6. STRESS MANAGEMENT

### 6.1 Stress Recognition

**Physiological Signs:**
- Increased heart rate
- Rapid breathing
- Sweating (palms, forehead)
- Muscle tension
- Dry mouth
- Butterflies in stomach

**Cognitive Effects:**
- Narrowed attention (tunnel vision)
- Memory impairment
- Slowed decision-making
- Rigidity (difficulty adapting)
- Premature closure (jumping to conclusions)

**Emotional Indicators:**
- Anxiety, fear
- Irritability, anger
- Feeling overwhelmed
- Loss of confidence

**Performance Degradation:**
- Increased errors
- Omitted steps
- Poor communication
- Fixation on one problem

### 6.2 Stress Management Techniques

**Immediate (In-Flight):**
- **Breathing**: Deep, controlled breaths (box breathing: 4 in, 4 hold, 4 out, 4 hold)
- **Task Prioritization**: Focus on highest priority (Aviate first)
- **Task Shedding**: Defer non-critical tasks
- **Team Support**: Share workload, verbalize concerns
- **Reset**: Brief pause to collect thoughts (if time permits)

**Preparation (Training):**
- Realistic scenario training (exposure builds confidence)
- Emergency procedure practice (automaticity reduces stress)
- Decision-making exercises (improve speed and quality)
- CRM training (team support skills)
- Physical fitness (improves stress resilience)

**Long-Term:**
- Adequate rest and sleep
- Healthy lifestyle (diet, exercise)
- Work-life balance
- Social support networks
- Hobbies and stress relief activities

## 7. BWB-SPECIFIC HUMAN FACTORS

### 7.1 Non-Conventional Handling Awareness

**Different Cues:**
- Pitch response feels different from conventional aircraft
- Roll inertia higher (slower roll response)
- Wide field of view may affect distance perception
- Ground handling requires different visual references

**Adaptation:**
- Thorough training on BWB characteristics
- Gradual introduction (simulator, then aircraft)
- Regular practice to maintain proficiency
- Awareness of conventional aircraft habits

### 7.2 Wide CG Range Management

**Mental Model:**
- CG affects pitch control and stability more than conventional
- Wide CG envelope requires constant awareness
- CAOS assists but crew must understand fundamentals

**Procedural Support:**
- CAOS real-time CG display and management
- Enhanced crew CG awareness training
- Procedures emphasize CG verification
- Alerts for approaching CG limits

### 7.3 Evacuation Coordination

**Challenges:**
- Wider cabin (more area to cover)
- Multiple levels possible
- Distributed seating (less central control)
- Crew positioning critical

**Human Factors Solutions:**
- Clear crew positioning procedures
- Enhanced cabin communication
- Passenger briefing emphasizes nearest exit
- Crew training includes BWB-specific evacuation
- Drills practice BWB scenarios

## 8. H2 SYSTEM INTERACTION

### 8.1 Invisible Hazard Awareness

**Challenge:**
- H2 leaks not visibly apparent
- H2 flames nearly invisible in daylight
- Sensory cues limited (H2 odorless)

**Human Factors Solutions:**
- Rely on detection systems (trust but verify)
- Training includes H2 leak scenarios
- Use of IR cameras (for fire detection)
- Procedures emphasize detector readings
- Visual inspection secondary to instruments

### 8.2 Cryogenic Handling Precautions

**Awareness:**
- -253Â°C causes instant frostbite
- Even vapor can cause cold burns
- Materials become brittle

**Procedural Support:**
- Mandatory PPE (cryogenic gloves, face shield)
- No-touch zones clearly marked
- Training includes cryogenic safety
- Buddy system for H2 operations
- Emergency response for exposure

### 8.3 Leak Detection Trust

**Challenge:**
- Crew must trust system they can't verify visually
- False alarms may reduce trust
- Critical to respond correctly every time

**Trust Building:**
- Regular detector testing (demonstrates reliability)
- Training on detector operation and limits
- Clear procedures for different alert levels
- No "crying wolf" (false alarms investigated and fixed)
- Positive feedback when system performs correctly
